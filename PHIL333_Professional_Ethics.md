## 1.1—Descriptive vs Prescriptive Morality
Explain in your own words the main difference between descriptive morality and prescriptive morality, and provide an example of a descriptive ethical claim and an example of a normative ethical claim.

[top of the page](#top)	

### Descriptive Morality
Descriptive morality is like being a social scientist or a reporter. It involves describing the moral values, beliefs, and behaviors that individuals or groups actually have[cite: 1445, 1454]. When we use the term in this sense, we are simply stating facts about what people believe is right or wrong[cite: 1455]. These are "empirical" claims because they describe a part of reality that exists or could exist[cite: 1460, 1462].

* **Example of a descriptive ethical claim:** "John believes that employers should respect their employees"[cite: 1457]. This sentence does not say if John is right; it just reports the fact that she has that specific belief[cite: 1457]. Another example would be stating that "Canadians believe that all persons... deserve equal access to quality health-care services"[cite: 1458]. This is just a description of a popular belief held by a group[cite: 1458, 1459].

### Prescriptive Morality
Prescriptive morality (also known as normative ethics) is the core of philosophy. It does not just report on beliefs; it "prescribes" how people should act or what rules they must follow[cite: 1465, 1470]. This side of morality is about reaching for a "moral ideal"[cite: 1465]. When we make a prescriptive claim, we are judging an action as right or wrong based on that ideal[cite: 1466]. A key feature here is "universality," which is the idea that if a rule is right, it should be followed by every rational person in a similar situation[cite: 1474, 1475].

* **Example of a normative ethical claim:** "People should receive equal pay for equal work"[cite: 1467]. This claim does not report a poll or a person's opinion; it sets a standard of fairness that "ought" to be required in the workplace[cite: 1468, 1470].


### The Main Difference
The main difference is that descriptive morality focuses on "what is," often using words like "is" or "was" to describe facts[cite: 1463]. Prescriptive morality focuses on "what ought to be," often using words like "should," "may," or "must" to set requirements[cite: 1471, 1472]. They are independent of each other because what people *actually* do (descriptive) is not always what they *should* do (prescriptive)[cite: 1478, 1479].

To help you reflect on this, consider a professional you know, like a doctor or an accountant. If you describe their daily habits, are you doing descriptive or prescriptive ethics? How would your approach change if you started evaluating whether those habits were actually "good" for their clients?

## 1.2—Amorality
Provide a brief description of a scenario that you would consider to be amoral, and explain why that is so.

[top of the page](#top)	

An **immoral** act is a violation of a moral rule (like a lawyer lying to a judge). An **amoral** scenario, however, is one that sits completely outside the "moral bucket." It is an event or entity that cannot be judged as right or wrong because it lacks the capacity for moral thought or choice.

### The Scenario: A Landslide in the Rockies
Imagine a heavy rainstorm in the Rocky Mountains that causes a massive landslide. The rocks tumble down a hill and destroy an empty cabin that a family worked years to build. From a human perspective, this is a "bad" event because it causes loss and sadness. However, from a philosophical standpoint, the landslide itself is strictly **amoral**.



### Why is this amoral?
The reason we categorize this as amoral is the total absence of **moral agency**. As we see in the Rowan and Zinaich text, morality requires a "moral agent"—a being who can reason, understand consequences, and make a free choice. 

To see why the landslide is amoral, let’s look at it through our three core normative theories:

1.  **Utilitarianism**: A Utilitarian focuses on the "greatest good for the greatest number." While the landslide caused "disutility" (unhappiness) for the cabin owners, we cannot morally blame the rocks. The landslide didn't "choose" to decrease happiness; it simply followed the laws of gravity. Without an intentional actor trying to produce a result, the event has no moral weight.
2.  **Deontology (Kantianism)**: Kantian ethics focus on "duty" and "intent." For an action to be moral or immoral, the actor must have a "will." A pile of rocks has no will, no sense of duty, and cannot follow a moral law. Therefore, it is impossible for the rocks to "violate" a duty.
3.  **Contractarianism**: This theory suggests that morality is a "social contract" or agreement between rational beings to respect each other's rights. Since a mountain cannot enter into an agreement or sign a contract to respect property rights, it exists entirely outside the moral community.

## 1.3—Positive and Negative Rights

### Two Positive Rights (Not in the Unit)

1.  **The Right to a Safe Retirement (Pension):** This is a claim that society or an employer must provide financial support once a person is too old to work. Because this requires a "legitimate claim demanding that someone act" (by paying into a fund and distributing money), it is a positive right[cite: 1545].
2.  **The Right to Clean Drinking Water:** This isn't just about not polluting a stream (which would be a negative right). As a positive right, it is the claim that the government must build infrastructure and provide the service of delivering safe water to your home.

### Two Negative Rights (Not in the Unit)

1.  **Freedom of Religion:** This is the right to practice your faith without the government stopping you. It requires "only inaction on the part of others"[cite: 1550]. As long as no one interferes with your prayers or services, your right is satisfied.
2.  **Freedom of Assembly:** This is the right to gather in a group for a protest or a meeting. It is a negative right because it demands that the state "refrain from acting" to break up your peaceful gathering[cite: 1554].

Explain a situation where a professional has a prima facie duty that turns out not to be her actual duty, and explain another situation where a professional has a prima facie duty that does turn out to be her actual duty.

[top of the page](#top)	

A *prima facie* duty is a "duty at first sight." It is a moral obligation that we should always follow unless it conflicts with an even stronger duty. An *actual duty* is what you end up doing after you have weighed all your *prima facie* duties against each other in a specific situation.

### Situation 1: When a Prima Facie Duty is NOT the Actual Duty
Imagine a professional psychologist named Sarah. Sarah has a *prima facie* duty of **confidentiality**. This means she has a standing obligation to keep everything her clients say private. This is a core part of the "social contract" between a professional and a client.

However, during a session, a client tells Sarah that he plans to physically harm a specific coworker later that evening. Now, Sarah has a second *prima facie* duty: the **duty to prevent harm** (non-maleficence). 

In this case, the duty to protect a human life is more "stringent" (stronger) than the duty to keep a secret. Therefore, Sarah’s *prima facie* duty of confidentiality is overridden. Her **actual duty** is to break confidentiality and warn the police or the intended victim. Even though she broke a rule, she acted rightly because she followed the higher duty.



### Situation 2: When a Prima Facie Duty IS the Actual Duty
Now, let’s look at a different professional, an engineer named Marcus. Marcus has a *prima facie* duty of **fidelity**, which means he must follow through on his professional promises and contracts. He has been hired to design a bridge that meets specific safety standards.

As Marcus works, he finds no conflicting duties. There is no emergency, no threat to the public, and no reason to lie. Because there are no other duties "outweighing" his promise to the client, his *prima facie* duty to follow the contract remains his **actual duty**. In many daily professional tasks, our *prima facie* duties and actual duties are the same because no moral conflict has appeared.

## 1.5—Learn Before You Leap
Explain in your own words why it is important to understand another person’s reasoning before you critique that reasoning and before you offer counter-arguments. Demonstrate the importance of this practice with a brief example.

[top of the page](#top)	


### Why Understanding Comes First
It is vital to understand another person’s reasoning before critiquing it for two main reasons: **accuracy** and **respect**. 

First, from an intellectual standpoint, if you do not fully grasp the "why" behind someone’s position, you risk committing the "Straw Man" fallacy. This happens when you attack a weak or twisted version of their argument rather than the real one. Critiquing a misunderstanding does not move the conversation forward; it is essentially talking to yourself. 

Second, as Rowan and Zinaich emphasize, professionals are members of a **moral community**. This community is built on the idea that we are all rational beings. If you dismiss someone's view without understanding their logic, you are failing to treat them as a rational "end in themselves" (a key Deontological concept). You are treating them as an obstacle to be moved rather than a person to be heard.



### A Practical Example: The Whistleblower
Imagine an accountant, David, who discovers his firm is using a legal but aggressive tax loophole. A junior colleague, Sarah, argues they should report this to the authorities (whistleblowing). 

If David critiques Sarah immediately, he might say, "You just want to get the firm in trouble because you're unhappy here." This is a critique of her *motives*, not her *reasoning*. If David stops to understand her reasoning first, he might find that Sarah is worried about the **long-term public trust** in the accounting profession. She believes that if the public loses faith in accountants, the entire "social contract" of the profession fails. 

By understanding that Sarah's reasoning is based on **Contractarianism** (the health of the professional-social agreement), David can now offer a real counter-argument. He might argue from a **Utilitarian** perspective that reporting a legal act would cause more harm (job losses) than good, or he might offer a different interpretation of their contract with the public. Because he understood her first, the debate is now professional and productive rather than a personal attack.

## 2.1—Act vs Rule Utilitarianism
Provide an example of a morally problematic situation and explain how a proponent of act utilitarianism would reason about that situation differently than a proponent of rule utilitarianism.

[top of the page](#top)	


### The Problematic Situation: The Hospital Deception
Consider a situation described in Unit 2: A nurse is caring for a mother who has just been in a car accident[cite: 2299]. The mother is in great distress and repeatedly asks about her children. One child has actually died, but the doctor orders the nurse to lie and say everyone is fine for the night so the mother can stabilize before hearing the news the next day[cite: 2304].

### Reasoning with Act Utilitarianism
A proponent of **Act Utilitarianism** looks at this specific moment as a unique event. They act like a "utility calculator" for this one case[cite: 1978, 2319]. 

* **Calculation**: They weigh the immediate physical health of the mother against the temporary emotional pain of being lied to[cite: 2321]. 
* **The Decision**: If the nurse believes that telling the truth right now would cause the mother to have a heart attack or go into shock, the Act Utilitarian might decide that lying is the "right" thing to do[cite: 1979]. They only care about which of the available choices produces the most happiness (or least pain) *right now* in this specific hospital room[cite: 2320].

### Reasoning with Rule Utilitarianism
A proponent of **Rule Utilitarianism** does not look at the specific mother or doctor. Instead, they ask: "What if 'Lying to patients to prevent distress' became a general rule for all nurses?" [cite: 1995, 2334]

* **The Rule**: They look at the long-term consequences of that rule. If nurses generally lied to patients, the public would lose all trust in the medical profession[cite: 2336, 2431]. People would be afraid to go to hospitals, which would lead to a massive decrease in overall society-wide happiness.
* **The Decision**: Since a rule of "always tell the truth" creates more utility for society in the long run than a rule of "lie when it seems helpful," the Rule Utilitarian would argue the nurse **must** tell the truth[cite: 2337]. Even if the mother is devastated in this one instance, the professional must follow the rule that produces the most good for everyone over time[cite: 2000].


### The Key Difference
The Act Utilitarian is looking for a "short-term fix" to maximize happiness in one situation, while the Rule Utilitarian is looking to maintain a "standard of behavior" that protects society's happiness in the long term[cite: 1998, 1999].


### q022 
## 2.2—Kant’s Concept of Duty
Do you agree with Kant’s reasoning that the concept of duty captures what is most important about ethics and about evaluating people’s moral behaviour, and why or why not?

[top of the page](#top)	

Immanuel Kant argued that the only thing in the world that is "good without qualification" is a **Good Will**. For Kant, a person is acting morally only when they act out of a sense of duty, rather than out of emotion, self-interest, or the desire for a specific result.

Whether one agrees that duty is the "most important" aspect of ethics depends on which philosophical lens you prioritize. Let’s break down the arguments for and against Kant’s position using our analytical framework.

### The Argument For: Why Duty is Central
Proponents of Kant’s reasoning (Deontologists) argue that duty is the most important part of ethics because it provides **consistency** and **respect**. 

Kant’s **Categorical Imperative** tells us to act only on rules that we would want to become universal laws. This prevents us from making "special exceptions" for ourselves. In professional ethics, this is vital. For example, an engineer has a duty to ensure the safety of a bridge. This duty is not based on how the engineer feels that day or whether the bridge will be profitable. It is a strict moral requirement. By focusing on duty, we treat people as **"ends in themselves"** rather than just tools (means) to get what we want.



### The Argument Against: The Limitations of Duty
Critics, such as **Utilitarians**, argue that focusing only on duty is "moral coldness." They believe the **consequences** of an action are what truly matter. If following a duty (like always telling the truth) leads to a predictable disaster (like a murderer finding their victim), a Utilitarian would say the "duty-bound" person acted immorally by ignoring the pain they caused.

**Contractarians** would offer another critique. They would argue that morality is not an internal sense of duty, but rather a **social agreement**. From this view, what is most important is not a person's inner "will," but whether they follow the rules that rational people agreed upon to ensure a functional society.

### Evaluating Moral Behavior
Is duty the best way to evaluate someone? If a doctor saves a patient because they have a duty to do so, Kant says that is a high moral act. But if a doctor saves a patient because they truly care about the person’s well-being, Kant says that action has less "moral worth" because it was done out of inclination (emotion) rather than duty. 

Many people find this hard to accept. We often value "good-hearted" people who act out of love or empathy more than those who act simply because "the manual says so." However, in a professional setting, **duty is more reliable than emotion**. Emotions change, but a professional duty remains constant.

## 2.3—Deontological vs Utilitarian Reasoning
Consider the hypothetical situation where you tell your son falsely that his mother loved him, which was used in this unit to illustrate Kantian deontological reasoning. Do you think that one of the utilitarian approaches or the Kantian approach better indicates the morally right action in this situation? Why?

[top of the page](#top)	

This hypothetical case—lying forces us to choose between two deeply held values: the duty to tell the truth and the desire to prevent unnecessary suffering. 
To determine which approach "better" indicates the morally right action, we must look at what each theory values most.

### The Kantian (Deontological) Approach
From a Kantian perspective, the answer is clear: you must not lie. Kant’s reasoning is based on the **Categorical Imperative**. He would argue that if you made "lying to spare feelings" a universal law, the very concept of truth and trust would collapse. If everyone lied to make others feel better, no one would ever believe a compliment or an expression of love again.

Furthermore, Kant emphasizes the **Formula of Humanity**. By lying to the son, you are treating him as a "means" to an end (his own comfort) rather than as a rational "end in himself." You are essentially deciding for him that he cannot handle the truth. This denies him his **autonomy**—his right to know the reality of his own life so he can process it as a rational person. To Kant, the truth is a "perfect duty" that cannot be set aside for the sake of happiness.

### The Utilitarian Approach
A proponent of **Act Utilitarianism** would likely argue that lying is the "right" action. They would calculate the "utility": 
1.  **The Lie**: Results in the son feeling loved, secure, and happy. 
2.  **The Truth**: Results in trauma, low self-esteem, and lifelong grief. 
Since the lie produces the "greatest happiness for the greatest number" (in this small circle), the Act Utilitarian sees it as a moral victory.

However, a **Rule Utilitarian** might hesitate. They would ask: "What if all parents lied to children about their family history?" This could lead to a society where people discover their whole lives were a lie later on, leading to a massive breakdown in social trust and much more "disutility" in the long run.

While the Utilitarian approach feels more "humane" in the short term, the **Kantian approach** arguably provides a better moral indicator for **Professional Ethics**. In professions like Law or Medicine, we rely on the "informed consent" of the client. If a professional lied "for the client's own good," they would be violating the client's rights and dignity. 

In the case of the son, although the truth is painful, it respects him as a person. Protecting someone from the truth is often a form of "paternalism"—treating an adult (or a maturing child) like they are incapable of handling reality. By telling the truth, you acknowledge his status as a moral agent who has a right to his own history, however messy it may be.

## 2.4—Contractarianism
Do you agree with the basic contractarian idea that people should be bound to abide by only those moral principles to which they would rationally and freely agree? Why or why not?

[top of the page](#top)	

This theory, often associated with Thomas Hobbes and more recently John Rawls, suggests that morality is essentially a "social contract." In the PHIL 333 curriculum, we look at this through the lens of professional responsibility: the idea that the rules of a profession are an agreement between the professional and the public.

### The Argument for Contractarianism
The idea that we should only be bound by rules we would "rationally and freely" agree to is very powerful. It is based on the value of **autonomy**. If a rule is forced upon you and it doesn't make sense or benefit you, why should you follow it?

1.  **Mutual Benefit**: Rational people agree to rules (like "don't steal") because they want to live in a world where their own stuff isn't stolen. It turns morality into a "win-win" situation.
2.  **Fairness (The Veil of Ignorance)**: As we see in the work of John Rawls, a "fair" contract is one we would agree to if we didn't know who we would be in society—rich or poor, talented or not. This ensures that the rules don't unfairly favor one group over another.



### The Critique of Contractarianism
While this sounds fair, there are significant philosophical concerns. 
* **The Vulnerable**: If morality is only a contract between "rational and free" people, what happens to those who cannot agree to a contract? This includes infants, people with severe cognitive disabilities, or even animals. If they aren't "parties to the contract," does that mean we have no moral duties toward them? 
* **Self-Interest**: Critics argue that Contractarianism is just "calculated selfishness." It suggests we only do the right thing because it helps us, not because it is inherently "good."

### Applying our Normative Framework
To help you evaluate this for your **Philosophy Research Paper (Assignment 2)**, let's see how our other theories might react to this "agreement" idea:

* **Deontology (Kantianism)**: Kant would actually find some common ground here. He believed that moral laws must be ones that every rational person could stand behind. However, he would argue that duty comes from **reason itself**, not just from a "deal" we made with others to stay safe.
* **Utilitarianism**: A Utilitarian might disagree with the "free agreement" part. If a rule produces the **greatest happiness for the greatest number**, a Utilitarian would say you must follow it even if you didn't personally "agree" to it. For them, the results (utility) matter more than the agreement.

### My Perspective as your Professor
In professional ethics, the contractarian idea is very useful. It explains why professionals have "codes of conduct." These codes are the "terms of service" for being a doctor, engineer, or accountant. However, relying *only* on a contract can be dangerous. If a situation arises that isn't "in the contract," a professional still needs a sense of duty (Deontology) or a concern for the outcome (Utilitarianism) to do the right thing.

## Study Question Forums 2.5—Rawlsian Justice
Do you think Rawls’ two principles of justice provide adequate moral justification for the social inequalities that would exist in a Rawlsian society, given that some people, and not others, would achieve high levels of income and status within society, even while they were constrained by the principle of equal liberty? Explain your reasoning.

[top of the page](#top)	

To understand whether John Rawls’ two principles of justice provide an adequate justification for inequality, we must first revisit the "Original Position." In our **PHIL 333** studies, this is the central thought experiment of **Contractarianism**. Rawls asks us to imagine we are behind a "Veil of Ignorance," where we do not know our social status, wealth, or natural talents. Under these conditions, Rawls argues we would choose two specific principles to govern society.

### The Two Principles of Justice
1.  **The Principle of Equal Liberty:** This guarantees everyone the same basic rights and freedoms (like speech and assembly). This principle has "lexical priority," meaning you cannot trade your freedom for more money or status.
2.  **The Principle of Social and Economic Inequality:** This has two parts. First, positions of high status must be open to everyone under "fair equality of opportunity." Second, the **Difference Principle** states that inequalities are only allowed if they result in the "greatest benefit to the least-advantaged members of society."



### Is the Justification Adequate?
The argument that these principles *do* provide a moral justification is based on the idea of **mutual benefit**. In a Rawlsian society, we allow some people (like surgeons or innovative engineers) to earn much higher incomes. Why? Because the high pay acts as an incentive. If surgeons didn't get paid well, fewer people might go through the grueling years of training. If having highly-paid surgeons means that even the poorest person in society gets better healthcare than they would in a "perfectly equal" society where no one wanted to be a surgeon, then the inequality is justified.

From a **Contractarian** perspective, this is a fair deal. If you were behind the Veil of Ignorance, you would likely agree to a system where some people are richer than you, provided that their wealth actually makes your life as a "least-advantaged" person better than it would be otherwise.

### Critiquing the Justification
However, some philosophers argue this justification is not entirely adequate. 

* **Utilitarianism:** A Utilitarian might argue that Rawls is too focused on the "least advantaged." If a different system created a slightly worse life for the very poorest but a massive increase in happiness for the middle and upper classes, a Utilitarian might prefer that system because it produces a higher "total utility."
* **Deontology (Kantianism):** A Kantian might worry about the "status" aspect. If extreme wealth allowed some people to have more political power or influence, could we still say that the poor are being treated as "ends in themselves"? If the gap in status becomes too large, it might undermine the first principle of **Equal Liberty**, as the wealthy might find ways to "buy" more influence than the poor.

### My Perspective as a Professor
Rawls’ framework is powerful because it recognizes that humans are often motivated by rewards, but it forces those rewards to serve a moral purpose. It turns "selfish" ambition into a tool for social good. However, the adequacy of this justification depends on how we define "benefit." Does "benefit" only mean money and healthcare, or does it include the psychological impact of being at the bottom of a high-status ladder?

## 2.9—Normative Ethical Theories I
In your own words, identify the main features of each of the five normative ethical theories you have studied in this unit (six theories when we count act and rule utilitarianism as separate theories). To help you master the material for PHIL 333, we can break down these six normative theories into easy-to-understand concepts. These theories provide the frameworks that professionals use to decide what is right or wrong in the workplace.

### 1. Act Utilitarianism
Act Utilitarianism is a "consequentialist" theory, meaning it only cares about the results of an action. When you face a moral problem, this theory asks you to calculate which specific action will produce the greatest amount of happiness for the greatest number of people. It is very flexible; for example, if lying in one specific situation saves many lives, an act utilitarian would say lying is the right thing to do in that moment.



### 2. Rule Utilitarianism
Rule Utilitarianism also focuses on happiness, but it looks at the long-term. Instead of looking at every single act, it asks: "What general rule would create the most happiness if everyone followed it?" Even if lying might help in one specific case, a rule utilitarian might say we should "always tell the truth" because a society where no one can trust each other would be very unhappy in the long run.

### 3. Deontology (Kantianism)
Deontology, often associated with Immanuel Kant, is about duty and rules rather than consequences. It argues that some actions are just plain right or wrong, regardless of the outcome. A key tool here is the "Categorical Imperative," which says you should only act on rules that you would want to become a universal law for everyone. If you wouldn't want everyone to steal, then you have a strict duty never to steal.

Contractarianism (or Social Contract Theory) suggests that morality is a set of rules that rational people agree to follow for their mutual benefit. We agree not to harm others as long as they agree not to harm us. In professional ethics, this theory helps us understand why we have codes of conduct—they are "contracts" we sign to ensure everyone can work together safely and fairly.

Unlike the theories above that focus on *actions*, Virtue Ethics focuses on the *person*. It asks: "What kind of person should I be?" By practicing virtues like honesty, courage, and fairness, you develop a good character. The idea is that a virtuous person will naturally know the right thing to do because they have developed the right habits.

### 6. Feminist Ethics (Care Ethics)
Feminist ethics focuses on the importance of relationships and the power balance between people. It criticizes other theories for being too cold or logical. This theory argues that we must consider how our decisions affect vulnerable people and prioritize "care" and empathy. It reminds professionals to look at the social structures and power gaps that might make a situation unfair for some.



By understanding these six approaches, you can look at any professional dilemma—like a conflict of interest or a whistleblowing case—from multiple angles.

## Study Question Forums 2.10—Normative Ethical Theories II
Explain in your own words one main objection to each of the five normative ethical theories studied in this unit.

[top of the page](#top)	

In our study of PHIL 333, we have looked at how these theories help us make professional decisions. However, every philosophical framework has its weaknesses. Understanding these objections is just as important as understanding the theories themselves, as it helps you recognize the limitations of each approach when you are analyzing case studies for your assignments.

### 1. Utilitarianism: The "Justice and Rights" Objection
The biggest problem with utilitarianism is that it can sometimes justify doing something very unfair to a single person if it benefits a large group. Because it only looks at the total amount of happiness, it doesn't have a strong way to protect individual rights. For example, in a medical setting, a utilitarian might technically be able to justify sacrificing one healthy person to harvest their organs and save five others. This "ends justify the means" logic feels morally wrong to many because it treats people as tools rather than as individuals with their own rights.



### 2. Deontology: The "Conflicting Duties" Objection
Deontology tells us to follow universal rules, like "always tell the truth." The main objection here is that it can be too rigid and fails when two duties crash into each other. If you are a lawyer and you have a duty to keep a client's secret (confidentiality), but you also have a duty to prevent harm to the public, deontology doesn't always provide a clear way to choose between them. Kantian ethics famously suggests you should never lie, even to a murderer looking for their victim, which many people find logically and morally hard to accept.



### 3. Contractarianism: The "Exclusion" Objection
Contractarianism says morality is a deal made between rational people for their own benefit. The major objection is that this excludes anyone who cannot "negotiate" or offer something in return. This includes animals, the environment, very young children, and people with severe cognitive disabilities. If morality is just a contract between equals, then those who are not "equal" or "rational contractors" might not be protected by moral rules, leaving the most vulnerable members of society without rights.



### 4. Virtue Ethics: The "Lack of Guidance" Objection
Virtue ethics focuses on being a good person, but the main criticism is that it doesn't give you a clear "to-do list" when things get complicated. If you are a young engineer facing a whistleblowing dilemma, telling you to "be courageous" or "be honest" isn't very helpful if you don't know which virtue is more important in that specific moment. Critics argue that virtue ethics is too vague and circular; it tells you to do what a virtuous person would do, but you only know someone is virtuous because they do the right things.

### 5. Feminist Ethics: The "Partiality" Objection
Feminist ethics (or Care Ethics) emphasizes the importance of personal relationships and caring for those close to us. The objection here is that this can lead to "partiality" or favoritism. If we only focus on the people we have a relationship with, we might ignore our duties to people far away or to society as a whole. In a professional setting, if an accountant prioritizes "caring" for a friend who is a client, they might fail in their larger duty to be objective and fair to the public and the government.

### The Descriptive Account: Ernest Greenwood
A descriptive account, like the one provided by **Ernest Greenwood**, acts like a scientific observation. It describes "what is." Greenwood looks at the social structures and characteristics that existing professions (like law and medicine) actually possess. He provides a "checklist" of five attributes that distinguish a profession from other occupations:

4.  **Ethical Codes:** The profession has a formal set of rules that members must follow.
5.  **Professional Culture:** The group shares specific values, norms, and symbols.

This approach is "value-laden" because it places the moral relationship between the professional and the public at the very center of the definition.



### The Key Difference
The main difference is the **starting point**. Greenwood’s descriptive account looks from the "outside in." He looks at the external signs of a profession, such as its power and organization. Ernest Greenwood identifies five specific "attributes" that a profession must possess to a high degree[cite: 2479].
1.  **A Systematic Body of Theoretical Knowledge:** Professionals do not just have practical skills; their work is based on an extensive, often abstract, body of theory usually acquired through years of formal higher education[cite: 2507, 2510].
2.  **Professional Authority:** Because of their specialized knowledge, professionals are seen as authorities. Clients are often in a vulnerable position and must subordinate their own judgment to the professional's expertise[cite: 2527, 2543, 2545].
3.  **Sanction of the Community:** A profession is granted a monopoly over its services by society. This "social bargain" allows the profession to control its own training, accreditation, and licensing in exchange for a commitment to public service[cite: 2554, 2565, 2567].
4.  **A Regulative Code of Ethics:** Professions have formalized, public codes of conduct that ensure practitioners act in the interest of the public rather than purely out of self-interest[cite: 2572, 2574, 2585].

Instead of looking for a strict list of features that every profession *must* have, Downie argues that professions share a set of overlapping traits[cite: 2497].

For Downie, no single trait is absolutely necessary or sufficient to define a profession on its own[cite: 2498].

These two accounts **mostly agree** with one another[cite: 1021, 2494]. Both Greenwood and Downie reject the idea that there is a simple, black-and-white definition of a profession. They both acknowledge that the concept is "slippery" and that the boundaries between a profession and a non-profession are often fuzzy[cite: 1021, 1034, 1138].

However, there is a significant point of difference in their perspective:
* **Greenwood’s account is descriptive.** He aims to list the attributes that actually exist in the world to describe how a profession functions as a social entity[cite: 2641].
* **Downie’s account is evaluative.** While he recognizes the same types of traits, his focus is more on the underlying nature and the values attached to these roles[cite: 2641].

In summary, while Greenwood provides a more structured list of attributes and Downie offers a more flexible "resemblance" model, both agree that professional status is defined by a combination of specialized knowledge, public trust, and a shared ethical commitment.

## 3.7—Professional Responsibility I

In the study of professional ethics, one of the most fundamental debates concerns the source and limit of a professional's duties. This debate is usually framed as a conflict between **Legalism** and **Anti-legalism**. Both positions attempt to answer the question: "Is following the rules enough to be an ethical professional?"

### The Basic Legalist Position

Legalism is the view that professional responsibility is defined entirely by the law and the formal "Codes of Ethics" established by professional associations. From a legalist perspective, if an action is legal and does not violate a specific rule in the professional code, then it is morally permissible. 

The primary argument for legalism is **clarity and objectivity**. In a complex world, professionals need clear boundaries. By sticking to the "letter of the law," a professional ensures that they are acting in a predictable way that society has already agreed upon. This limits "arbitrary judgment," where a professional might impose their own personal values on a client. For a legalist, the code of ethics serves as a complete map of their moral obligations. If something isn't in the code, the professional has no special duty to do it.



### The Basic Anti-legalist Position

Anti-legalism, on the other hand, argues that professional responsibility goes far beyond just following rules. Anti-legalists believe that codes of ethics are often incomplete, vague, or even contradictory. They argue that rules provide only a **"moral minimum"**—the bare essentials of behavior—but do not capture the full meaning of being a professional.

For the anti-legalist, the core of professionalism is **discretionary judgment**. Since every client and situation is unique, a professional cannot simply follow a manual. They must understand the "spirit" of their profession and the values it serves, such as justice in law or health in medicine. Being an ethical professional requires "moral character" and the ability to think critically about the impact of one's actions on the public good, even when there is no specific rule to guide them.

### Critical Analysis through Normative Theories

To evaluate these positions, we can apply our core philosophical frameworks:

1.  **Utilitarianism:** A legalist might argue that following rules maximizes utility because it creates a stable, efficient system. However, an anti-legalist would point out that strictly following a rule in a weird situation could lead to a disastrous outcome. To achieve the **greatest good for the greatest number**, professionals must sometimes look past the rules to ensure the best consequences.
2.  **Deontology (Kantianism):** Kantian ethics focuses on **duty and intent**. While legalism focuses on the "external" act of following a rule, a Deontologist would argue that the "internal" reason for the action matters. If a professional follows a rule only to avoid a fine, they aren't truly being moral. One has a duty to respect the humanity of the client, which often requires more than just following a written code.
3.  **Contractarianism:** This view sees the profession as a **"social bargain."** Society gives the profession power, and in return, the profession promises to protect the public. An anti-legalist would argue that this bargain is a moral contract, not just a legal one. If a professional uses a legal "loophole" to harm the public, they have broken the social contract, even if they didn't technically break a rule.

In conclusion, while legalism provides a helpful framework for basic behavior, the anti-legalist position is generally seen as more robust in PHIL 333. It acknowledges that being a professional requires an active commitment to ethical values and the wisdom to apply them when the rulebook falls short.

Interpretative obedience is the act of following a rule by understanding its purpose, its context, and how it is understood by other members of the same profession. It is not "blind obedience," where a person follows the literal words without thinking about the consequences. Nor is it "malicious obedience," where a person follows a rule specifically to cause a problem or to show how "stupid" the rule is.

Instead, interpretative obedience requires **professional competence**. For example, if a code of ethics says an engineer must "protect the safety of the public," a person practicing interpretative obedience doesn't just look for a definition of "safety" in a standard dictionary. Instead, they look at how the engineering profession defines safety, what the current technical standards are, and how a "reasonable engineer" would interpret that duty in a complex, real-world situation.

[Image comparing blind, malicious, and interpretative obedience]

### Why It Satisfies Professional Responsibility

Davis believes that interpretative obedience satisfies professional responsibility for several key reasons:

1.  **The Code as a Social Contract:** Following the Rowan and Zinaich framework, a profession is based on a "social bargain." The profession promises to provide a necessary good (like justice, health, or safety) in exchange for high status and autonomy. For Davis, the Code of Ethics is the written form of this promise. By following the code interpretatively, the professional is literally fulfilling the contract they made with society.

2.  **Consistency and Fairness:** If every professional used only their own personal "inner compass" to decide what is right, the public would never know what to expect. Interpretative obedience ensures that all members of a profession are aiming for the same standards. This creates a reliable "system of trust" where the public can count on a certain level of behavior from any licensed professional.

3.  **Avoiding Personal Bias:** Interpretative obedience forces professionals to look outside their own personal opinions and toward the shared values of their field. This helps prevent "conflicts of interest" or personal biases from clouding their judgment. By sticking to the professional interpretation of the rules, they ensure they are acting as a representative of their profession, not just as an individual.

4.  **Moral Weight of the Rules:** Davis argues that these rules are not just "administrative" or "bureaucratic" hoops to jump through. Because they are designed to protect the public and ensure the profession functions correctly, following them correctly (interpretatively) is a moral act. It is the primary way a professional exercises their "fiduciary duty" to the client and the community.

In the PHIL 333 curriculum, this concept is vital because it bridges the gap between **Legalism** (just following the law) and **Anti-legalism** (doing whatever you think is right). Davis shows that a professional can be both a "rule-follower" and a deeply ethical, autonomous thinker at the same time. By interpreting the rules through the lens of professional expertise, the practitioner ensures that the "spirit" of the code is upheld, which is the ultimate goal of professional responsibility.

Don Welch’s argument that professional ethics are not distinct from ordinary ethics is a central debate in our curriculum. This is often called the **"Unitary View"** of ethics. Welch argues that we do not need a special set of moral rules for professionals. Instead, he believes that the same moral principles that guide us in our daily lives—such as honesty, fairness, and avoiding harm—are exactly the same principles that should guide a doctor, lawyer, or engineer. While his argument is logically consistent, many students find it unconvincing because it seems to overlook the unique "moral space" that professionals occupy.

### The Argument for Welch (The Unitary View)

Welch’s position is convincing if you look at it through a **Deontological (Kantian)** lens. Immanuel Kant argued that morality is based on "Categorical Imperatives"—rules that must apply to everyone, everywhere, regardless of their job. From this perspective, a lie is a lie, whether it is told by a used-car salesman or a physician. If we believe that human beings have a universal duty to respect others, then a professional’s duty is simply a specific application of that general human duty.

Similarly, from a **Utilitarian** perspective, the goal is always to produce the "greatest good for the greatest number." Whether you are acting as a private citizen or a professional accountant, your moral "math" remains the same: you should choose the action that results in the most happiness and the least suffering. In this view, professional codes are just "rules of thumb" that help us apply ordinary utilitarian ethics more quickly in a workplace setting.



### The Argument Against Welch (The Role-Differentiated View)

However, I find Welch’s argument less convincing when we consider the **Contractarian** framework and the concept of **role-differentiated duties**. A "profession" is built on a "social bargain" where society grants a group special privileges (like a monopoly on practice) in exchange for the group's commitment to higher, specific standards.

In many cases, a professional is actually *required* to do things that would be considered immoral in ordinary life. For example:
* **Confidentiality:** If a friend tells you they committed a crime, ordinary ethics might suggest you should tell the authorities. However, a lawyer has a "special" professional duty to keep that information secret to protect the legal system's integrity.
* **Neutrality:** A doctor must treat a wounded criminal with the same care as a wounded hero. In ordinary life, we might feel a moral urge to favor the "good" person, but professional ethics demands a specific type of impartiality.

### Conclusion

Welch would argue that these aren't "new" ethics, but just ordinary ethics (like keeping a promise) applied to a specific professional promise. However, this seems to downplay how much a professional’s role can change their moral landscape. If a professional role can turn a "wrong" act (like keeping a secret about a crime) into a "right" act (protecting attorney-client privilege), it is hard to argue that the ethics are exactly the same. 

To truly master this unit, consider this: If we follow Welch and say there is no "special" professional ethics, do we risk making professional codes of conduct seem optional or less important? Or does his view actually make professionals *more* accountable by reminding them that they cannot hide behind a "professional" excuse to do something that is fundamentally inhumane? Understanding this tension is key to analyzing the cases we will study in the applied ethics units.

The most significant disadvantage is a **lack of credibility**. Information from an unknown source is often viewed with suspicion. Without a name attached, the report carries less weight in a legal or professional setting.

1.  **Utilitarianism:** A Utilitarian would weigh the "utility" (happiness/safety) produced by the report against the "disutility" (harm). 2.  **Deontology (Kantianism):** This theory focuses on **duty and truth**. A professional has a "prima facie" duty to be honest. While whistleblowing fulfills the duty to protect human life, Kant might ask if we can "universalize" anonymous reporting. 3.  **Contractarianism:** This view looks at the **social contract** or bargain. Professionals receive a monopoly on their work in exchange for protecting the public. If an organization is violating this contract, the professional has a duty to speak up. However, the contract also assumes a level of fairness. Is it fair to the accused organization to be attacked by a "faceless" accuser who cannot be cross-examined? Contractarians would seek a system where the whistleblower is protected but the information is still verifiable.

In summary, while anonymous whistleblowing is a vital safety net for professionals in dangerous situations, its lack of transparency can weaken its impact. De George argues that whistleblowing moves from being merely permissible to being **morally obligatory** if two additional conditions are satisfied:
4.  **Evidence:** The whistleblower has documented evidence that would convince a reasonable, impartial observer that their view of the threat is correct.
5.  **Success and Prevention:** There is strong evidence that making the information public will actually prevent the threatened harm and that the potential success is worth the personal risk.



### Mike Martin: The Prima Facie Obligation

While De George focuses on a step-by-step procedural approach, Mike Martin (often writing with Roland Schinzinger) uses a "Rescue" or "Good Samaritan" model. He argues that a professional has a **strong prima facie obligation** to blow the whistle when four conditions converge:
1.  **Need:** There is a clear and serious threat to someone's life, health, or fundamental well-being.
2.  **Proximity:** The professional is in a position to have firsthand knowledge of the threat and is "close" to the situation.
3.  **Capability:** The professional has the ability to act and a reasonable chance of effectively stopping the harm.
4.  **Last Resort:** No one else is more likely to help, or all other avenues for assistance have been exhausted.

### Philosophical Analysis through Normative Theories

To analyze these conditions, consider how our core theories would view them:

* **Utilitarianism:** A Utilitarian would strongly support De George’s fifth condition (likelihood of success). If blowing the whistle will only ruin the professional's life without actually saving the public, the total "utility" decreases. Therefore, the obligation only exists when the benefit to the public (the many) outweighs the harm to the whistleblower (the one).
* **Deontology (Kantianism):** From a Kantian perspective, the duty to protect human life is a "categorical imperative." Martin’s focus on "Need" and "Proximity" aligns with the idea that we have a fundamental duty to treat others as ends in themselves. If you have the "Capability" to save a life and fail to do so, you are violating your moral duty.
* **Contractarianism:** This view focuses on the **social bargain**. Society gives professionals a monopoly in exchange for public protection. De George’s "step-wise" approach respects the internal contract of the firm (loyalty) while acknowledging the higher contract with society. Martin’s "Last Resort" condition emphasizes that the professional only breaks the corporate contract when the social contract is in immediate danger.

## 4.11—Conflicts of Interest I
What is a conflict of interests and why are conflict of interest situations morally problematic for professionals in particular?

Also, explain the differences between real, potential, and apparent conflicts of interest.

[top of the page](#top)	


A **conflict of interest** occurs when a professional has a personal or private interest that is sufficient to influence the objective exercise of their professional duties. In the Rowan and Zinaich framework, this is not just about taking a bribe; it is about any situation where a professional’s "discretionary judgment" might be clouded by their own interests, such as family ties, financial gain, or personal friendships.

### Why It Is Morally Problemly for Professionals
Conflict of interest situations are specifically problematic for professionals because of the **fiduciary relationship**. Unlike a simple buyer-seller relationship where both sides look out for themselves, a professional has a special duty to act as a **faithful agent** for their client. The client is often in a vulnerable position and must trust the professional’s superior knowledge. If a professional has a conflict, they are essentially serving two masters: the client and themselves. This undermines the trust that is necessary for the profession to function.



### Three Types of Conflicts
The PHIL 333 curriculum distinguishes between three specific states of conflict:

1.  **Real Conflict of Interest:** This is a situation that currently exists where a professional’s judgment *is* being compromised. For example, a lawyer representing a client while also owning a large share of the opposing company is a real conflict. The professional is currently in a position where they cannot be objective.
2.  **Potential Conflict of Interest:** This is a situation that could *become* a conflict in the future. For example, an engineer might be working on a government project while also interviewing for a job with the private contractor hired to build it. While the engineer hasn't changed their judgment yet, the potential for a conflict is present.
3.  **Apparent Conflict of Interest:** This occurs when a reasonable outsider would *think* there is a conflict, even if there isn't one. For example, a judge having a private dinner with an old friend who happens to be the lawyer for one side in a trial creates an appearance of bias. Even if they never discuss the case, the public trust is damaged because it *looks* unfair.



### Philosophical Analysis
We can analyze these situations using our core normative theories:

* **Utilitarianism:** From this view, conflicts of interest are wrong because they lead to bad consequences for society. If the public loses trust in engineers or accountants, the entire economic and social system becomes less efficient. Protecting the "public good" requires professionals to remain unbiased to produce the greatest good for the greatest number.
* **Deontology (Kantianism):** This theory focuses on **duty**. A professional has a specific "role-differentiated duty" to be honest and loyal to their client. Using a client’s trust to benefit yourself treats the client as a "means to an end" (your own profit) rather than an "end in themselves."
* **Contractarianism:** This views professionalism as a **social bargain**. Society grants the professional a monopoly and high status in exchange for the promise of expert, unbiased service. A conflict of interest is a breach of this contract. If the professional isn't being objective, they aren't holding up their end of the deal.

However, the presence of a personal interest creates a powerful temptation that weakens their desire to do their duty.

They are taking the high pay and status granted by society without providing the unbiased protection they promised in return.



In conclusion, Resnik shows that conflicts of interest are not just minor mistakes; they are "moral poisons" that attack the very foundation of professional life. By distorting both the mind (judgment) and the heart (will), they destroy the trust that is essential for any professional to function effectively in society.

## 5.12—Patient Competency II
Explain why Buchanan and Brock think that, in assessing a patient’s decision-making competency, we must consider the particular choice made by the patient in relation to the alternatives the patient could choose. Do you agree with them, and why or why not?

In their influential work, Allen Buchanan and Dan Brock argue that competency is not a fixed, "all-or-nothing" state. Instead, they propose a **decision-relative** or **sliding-scale** standard for assessing a patient's decision-making capacity.

According to Buchanan and Brock, we cannot determine if a patient is competent without looking at the **particular choice** they are making and how that choice compares to the **available alternatives**.

### The Sliding Scale of Competence

The core of their argument is that the level of cognitive and evaluative capacity required to be deemed "competent" should vary based on the **stakes of the decision**. They suggest that the threshold for competence should rise as the risk of the decision increases.

* **Low-Stakes / Low-Risk:** If a patient agrees to a low-risk, highly beneficial treatment (like taking an antibiotic for a simple infection), the threshold for competence is set **low**. We don't need to perform an exhaustive psychiatric evaluation to let them consent to something clearly in their best interest.
* **High-Stakes / High-Risk:** If a patient refuses a life-saving treatment (like the dancer refusing the amputation in the previous example), the threshold for competence is set **very high**. Because the choice leads to a permanent and "bad" outcome compared to the alternatives, the physician must be extremely certain that the patient's reasoning process is flawless.



### Why We Must Consider Alternatives
Buchanan and Brock argue that focusing on the alternatives is necessary to balance the two primary values in medical ethics: **Beneficence** (acting in the patient's best interest) and **Respect for Autonomy** (respecting the patient's right to choose).

1.  **Risk-Benefit Ratio:** We judge a choice by looking at the "net benefit" of the chosen path versus the alternatives. If a patient chooses an alternative that results in a significant "net loss" of well-being, the professional's duty of beneficence is triggered. 
2.  **The "Safety Net" Function:** By raising the threshold of competence for high-risk refusals, we create a safety net. It allows us to protect patients from making impulsive or poorly understood decisions that have catastrophic, irreversible consequences.
3.  **Task-Specific Competence:** They argue that competence is always "competence to do $X$." Being competent to choose what to eat for lunch (Alternative A vs. B) is not the same as being competent to decide whether to undergo chemotherapy (Alternative C vs. D). The complexity of the task determines the level of ability required.

---

### Critical Analysis: Do I Agree?

I believe Buchanan and Brock’s model is **highly practical** but **philosophically dangerous**. Whether one agrees with them depends on which normative theory they prioritize.

#### Why it is a Strong Model (The Utilitarian View)
From a **Utilitarian** perspective, this model is excellent. It aims to minimize "disutility" (death or severe injury) by ensuring that people only make high-risk decisions when they are at their absolute cognitive best. It prevents the tragedy of an elderly patient with slight confusion accidentally "choosing" to die because they didn't quite grasp the alternatives. It produces the **greatest good** by protecting the vulnerable.

#### Why it is Problematic (The Deontological View)
A strict **Deontologist (Kantian)** might find this model deeply insulting to human dignity. If the "threshold" for my freedom moves based on whether the doctor likes my choice, do I really have autonomy? 
* Critics argue this is **"Paternalism in Disguise."** If I agree with the doctor, I am "competent" with very little effort. If I disagree, I am suddenly subjected to a much harder test. 
* This treats the patient’s rights as conditional. From a Kantian view, a person is either a rational agent or they are not; their status as a "chooser" shouldn't fluctuate based on the "risk" the doctor perceives.



### My Critical Conclusion
Ultimately, I **agree with Buchanan and Brock**, but only if the model is used as a **procedural safeguard**, not a way to force outcomes. 

In a perfect world, a "process standard" (looking only at how the patient thinks) would be enough. But in the messy reality of the hospital, patients are often tired, scared, or medicated. The sliding scale is a **Contractarian** necessity: society grants doctors the power to intervene because we want a system that errs on the side of life in cases of doubt. As long as the "high threshold" focuses on the **clarity of the patient's reasoning** rather than just the **content of their choice**, it remains a responsible way to handle the fiduciary duty.

## 5.13—Surrogate Decision Making
Explain why it is considered morally preferable for a surrogate decision maker to use the substituted judgment standard over the best interest standard. Develop an example and apply to it these two standards of surrogate decision making.

[top of the page](#top)	

In professional ethics, specifically within the healthcare context, **surrogate decision-making** is necessary when a patient lacks the capacity to make their own medical choices. Philosophers and bioethicists generally recognize a hierarchy of standards to guide surrogates, with **Substituted Judgment** considered morally superior to the **Best Interest Standard**.

### Why Substituted Judgment is Morally Preferable

The moral preference for substituted judgment is rooted in the principle of **Patient Autonomy**.

* **Respect for Persons (Deontology):** According to the Kantian view, we must treat individuals as "ends in themselves." Substituted judgment attempts to reach the decision the patient *would* have made if they were competent. By doing so, the surrogate acts as the patient’s "voice," honoring their unique values, religious beliefs, and life goals.
* **The Fiduciary Duty:** The professional's duty is to the patient’s specific interests, not just a generic idea of "health." Substituted judgment protects the patient’s **moral agency** even after they have lost the ability to speak, ensuring that their specific "life plan" is followed.
* **Extension of Self-Determination:** If we only used the Best Interest Standard, we would treat every patient like a "reasonable person" archetype. Substituted judgment recognizes that what is "good" for one person (e.g., prolonging life at all costs) might be a "harm" to another (e.g., someone who values bodily integrity over longevity).



---

### The Example: The Case of Elias

**The Scenario:** Elias is an 80-year-old man who has suffered a massive stroke and is now in a persistent vegetative state (PVS) with no hope of recovery. He has not left a formal "Living Will." However, his daughter, Sarah, is his surrogate. A decision must be made: **Should a feeding tube be surgically inserted to keep Elias alive indefinitely?**

**Elias’s Background:** Before his stroke, Elias was a devoutly religious man who often spoke about the "dignity of a natural death." He once watched a friend survive on a ventilator for years and told Sarah, "Don't ever let that happen to me; when the flame goes out, let it go out."

---

### Applying the Two Standards

#### 1. The Substituted Judgment Standard
Using this standard, Sarah asks: **"What would my father choose if he could sit up for five minutes and see this situation?"**
* **Application:** Sarah recalls her father's specific statements and his values regarding a "natural death." 
* **The Decision:** Sarah **refuses** the feeding tube. 
* **Moral Justification:** This decision honors Elias’s **autonomy**. It is a "substituted" version of his own will. Even though the decision leads to his death, it is the choice that is most consistent with the person Elias was.

#### 2. The Best Interest Standard
Using this standard, Sarah (or the medical team) asks: **"What would a reasonable person in this situation want, weighing the objective burdens and benefits?"**
* **Application:** The "benefit" is the continuation of life. The "burden" is a relatively minor surgery. A "reasonable person" generally wants to live, and there is no physical pain involved in being in a PVS.
* **The Decision:** Sarah **consents** to the feeding tube.
* **Moral Justification:** This decision is based on **beneficence**—doing what is objectively "good" for the patient's biological survival. It ignores Elias's personal "flame" comment because it wasn't a legal document, focusing instead on what society generally considers a positive outcome (preserving life).

---

### Philosophical Analysis

| Theory | Substituted Judgment View | Best Interest View |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Utilitarianism** | Focuses on the "utility" of the patient’s peace of mind (and their family's) by following their life's narrative. | Focuses on the "utility" of preserving life, often seen as an objective good. |
| **Deontology** | **Primary.** Respects the patient's specific rational will and autonomous history. | Secondary. Focuses on the duty of "non-maleficence" (not letting someone die). |
| **Contractarianism** | Fulfills the **social bargain** that the professional will protect the individual's rights against unwanted interference. | Fulfills the bargain that the professional will act as a "guardian" of human life in the absence of clear instructions. |

**Conclusion:** The Substituted Judgment Standard is the "ideal" because it keeps the patient at the center of the decision. The Best Interest Standard is a "safety net" used only when the surrogate has no idea what the patient’s values were. As a professional, your responsibility is to push for substituted judgment whenever possible to ensure that the patient’s **fiduciary trust** is not violated by an impersonal, "reasonable person" standard.

In short, **yes**, it is morally appropriate, and in many ways, it is already happening.

While all three professions have a **fiduciary duty**, Lichtenberg and other scholars point to several relevant differences that make public disclosure particularly vital—and uniquely possible—for journalists.

* **The Conflict:** Lawyers and doctors are bound by strict confidentiality (HIPAA or Attorney-Client Privilege). A lawyer who discloses that they receive income from "Client X" might be breaking the law or their professional oath.
* **The Journalist's Role:** Journalists do not have a similar "privilege" regarding their income sources. In fact, their professional integrity is built on **transparency**. While a lawyer’s power comes from their ability to keep secrets, a journalist’s power comes from their ability to reveal them.

#### 3. Regulation and Licensing
* **The Difference:** Doctors and lawyers are **licensed** by the state. If they act unethically, they can lose their license and their livelihood. 
* **The "Unregulated" Press:** Because of the First Amendment (in the US) and similar free-press principles elsewhere, there is no "license" for journalists. [top of the page](#top)	

In his influential article **"Moral Justifications for Privacy and Intimacy" (1996)**, Samuel Winch addresses the ethical tension between the public's "right to know" and an individual's right to be let alone. ### The Distinction Defined

Winch argues that not all private information carries the same moral weight. He divides privacy into two distinct spheres:

1.  **Intimate Privacy:** This refers to the most "sacred" and sensitive details of a person's life—those which are essential to human dignity, individual flourishing, and the development of close personal bonds. This includes information about one's **body, health, sexual orientation, inner thoughts, and closest familial or romantic relationships**. According to Winch, we have a near-absolute moral right to control this information because exposing it can cause deep psychological harm and disrupt our ability to form the intimate connections that make life meaningful.
2.  **Non-Intimate Privacy:** This covers personal information that is not part of the "inner core" of the self. It includes things like **financial records, daily routines, professional history, or general habits**. While this information is still personal and shouldn't be shared without a reason, it does not touch upon the fundamental "identity" of the person in the same way that intimate privacy does.



---

### Is This a Useful Moral Limit?

Winch argues that this distinction is crucial for placing a **moral limit** on journalistic intrusion, especially when dealing with public figures (politicians, celebrities, business leaders). 

* **The Limit:** For public figures, Winch suggests that the circle of **non-intimate** privacy is significantly smaller. Because these individuals hold power or public trust, their finances or past business dealings are often "fair game" for journalists if they relate to public interest.
* **The Barrier:** However, the circle of **intimate** privacy remains a firm barrier. Winch believes journalists are morally unjustified in exposing the intimate details of a public figure’s life *unless* that intimate behavior directly and significantly affects their public duties or reveals a profound character flaw relevant to their role.

### Evaluation and Reasoning

I agree with Winch that this distinction is a highly useful tool for professional ethics because it balances **fiduciary duty** with **human dignity**.

#### Why I Agree:
* **Deontology (Respect for Persons):** From a Kantian perspective, every person—including a public figure—must be treated as an "end in themselves." Stripping a public figure of their intimate privacy treats them as a mere "commodity" or "tool" for public entertainment. Winch’s distinction protects the basic human right to have a "backstage" where one can be a private person.
* **Utilitarianism (Social Utility):** A society that allows the total destruction of public figures' privacy will eventually struggle to attract talented, moral people into public service. If the "cost" of being a leader is the total exposure of one's body and soul, many good people will stay away. Protecting intimate privacy maximizes long-term social utility by maintaining a healthy pool of public servants.
* **Contractarianism (The Social Bargain):** The **social contract** for a public official usually involves trading some privacy for power (e.g., disclosing taxes). However, the bargain rarely includes the right for the public to know about one's most intimate health or relationship struggles unless they hinder the "job" being done. Winch’s model respects the boundaries of that original bargain.



#### Potential Critiques (Disagreement):
While useful, the distinction is increasingly difficult to apply in the modern era. Many public figures today (especially "influencers") intentionally monetize their **intimate** lives to gain public trust or fame. If a public figure uses their "intimacy" as a brand, can they still claim the protection of Winch's intimate privacy circle? Furthermore, the line between "intimate" and "relevant character flaw" is often subjective; for some, a politician's marital infidelity is a private intimate matter, while for others, it is a non-intimate indicator of their trustworthiness in public life.

Despite these challenges, Winch's framework provides a much-needed "ethical speed bump," forcing journalists to ask: *Is this information merely curious, or is it truly necessary for the public to know?*

By adopting this law, Thompson shifts the moral burden. He suggests that journalists cannot justify publishing a private detail simply by saying "the public is interested in it" or "it might reveal something about their character."

Often referred to as the **"Standard Conception"** of the legal profession, it suggests that a lawyer’s singular focus should be on advancing their client's interests within the bounds of the law, regardless of the lawyer’s personal opinion of the client or the morality of the client's goals.

### The Adversary System
To understand zealous advocacy, you have to look at the **adversary system**. This system is built on the belief that the best way to find the truth and achieve justice is to have two opposing sides present their strongest possible cases to a neutral third party (a judge or jury).

In this "battle" of arguments, the lawyer is the client’s champion. As philosophers like William Simon have noted, this conception of the lawyer's role rests on two controversial principles:

* **The Principle of Neutrality:** A lawyer must remain neutral toward the client's ends. They should not let their own moral, political, or social beliefs influence how they represent the client. Even if a lawyer finds a client’s business practices loathsome, their duty is to use the law to protect that client’s interests.
* **The Principle of Non-accountability:** As long as the lawyer stays within the legal rules, they are not morally responsible for the outcome of the case or the client’s behavior. If a lawyer helps a "guilty" person get acquitted by exposing a procedural error by the police, the lawyer is seen as having done their job well, not as having committed a moral wrong.

---

Why do we find this "hired gun" approach morally acceptable for professionals? We can look through our three core lenses:

* **Utilitarianism:** This theory argues that the adversary system, despite its flaws, produces the **greatest good for the greatest number** over the long run. By ensuring everyone has a zealous advocate, we prevent government overreach and protect individual rights, which leads to a more stable and just society.
* **Deontology (Kantianism):** From a Kantian perspective, zealous advocacy respects the **autonomy** and **dignity** of the client. The client is a rational agent with legal rights. If a lawyer refuses to zealously advocate for them based on personal distaste, they are treating the client as a "means" to their own moral comfort rather than as an "end in themselves."
* **Contractarianism:** This views the legal profession as a **social bargain**. Society grants lawyers a monopoly on legal service. In return, lawyers promise to provide a specific, unbiased service: the protection of the individual against the power of the state or larger entities. Zealous advocacy is the fulfillment of that "professional contract."

The main problem with this idea—and the reason it's a hot topic in our course—is that it can lead to **"adversarial overzealousness."** This happens when a lawyer uses legal loopholes to hide the truth, harass witnesses, or delay justice. 

The challenge for any "virtuous" lawyer is to determine where "zealous advocacy" ends and "obstruction of justice" begins. In the Rowan and Zinaich framework, the lawyer must balance their duty to the client with their **paramount duty** to the legal system itself.

An adversarial system, currently used in Canada and the United States, is defined by a competitive clash between two opposing sides[cite: 2551, 2553]. Key features include:
* **Competition:** Two lawyers compete to win by presenting opposing positions[cite: 452].
* **Neutral Arbiter:** A judge or jury determines which presentation is more convincing to reach a decision[cite: 452].
* **Truth through Conflict:** It assumes that this competitive process allows the truth to prevail, meaning the guilty are found guilty and the innocent are found not guilty[cite: 453, 454].
* **Zealous Advocacy:** Lawyers are expected to act as "zealous advocates," prioritizing their client's interests to win the case[cite: 216, 584].

### Basic Differences between Legal Systems

While the adversarial system relies on professional lawyers and a "winner-takes-all" competitive model, other systems offer different approaches to justice[cite: 2551].

#### Adversarial vs. Inquisitorial Systems
In an **inquisitorial system**, the focus shifts from a competition between two sides to an active investigation led by the court[cite: 2552]. 
* **Role of the Judge:** In an inquisitorial system, the judge plays a more active role in investigating the facts and "ferreting out truth"[cite: 738, 2552]. 
* **Professional Motivation:** Unlike the adversarial system, where the lawyers' primary motivation is to **win** for their clients, alternative systems like the inquisitorial one can functionally separate the search for truth from the individual motivations of the legal representatives[cite: 740, 868].

#### Adversarial vs. Convivial Systems
A **convivial system** represents a move away from professionalized legal control[cite: 2552]. 
* **Professional Monopoly:** The adversarial system is built on a "monopoly" of professional lawyers who claim to be the only ones competent to provide legal services[cite: 2548, 2553].
* **Accessibility:** A convivial system, by contrast, is more decentralized and less reliant on professional elites, allowing for legal services to be provided in a more straightforward manner[cite: 2552]. It contrasts with the "pure legal advocate" model where lawyers act as rote rule followers whose only goal is to win through technical legal rules[cite: 216, 217].

#### Adversarial vs. Free Market Systems
A society could also opt for a **free market system** where legal services are provided by non-professionals[cite: 2552]. Kipnis argues that if we do not choose these alternative systems and instead stick with an adversarial one, lawyers assume specific moral responsibilities to the public to justify their professional status and monopoly over the law[cite: 2548, 2553].

## 8.3—Auditing
According to Abdolmohammadi and Nixon, what is the difference between an ethics audit and a financial audit?

[top of the page](#top)	

According to Mohammad J. Abdolmohammadi and Mark R. Nixon, in their article "Ethics in the Public Accounting Profession," the primary difference between an ethics audit and a financial audit lies in their focus, the standards they apply, and the type of legitimacy they aim to establish for the profession.

### The Financial Audit: Legitimacy of Technique
A financial audit is the traditional, widely recognized process of examining a company's financial records. Its main purpose is to provide an expert and objective verdict on whether a company’s financial statements accurately represent its economic dealings in accordance with Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) or International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). 
* **Focus:** It centers on "technique" and numerical accuracy. The goal is to ensure that the rules of accounting are followed correctly.
* **Outcome:** The auditor issues one of four official opinions: clean, qualified, adverse, or a disclaimer. 
* **Public Role:** It provides assurance to third parties, such as investors and banks, allowing the market to function based on reliable data. Abdolmohammadi and Nixon refer to this as the **"legitimacy of technique,"** where the profession is trusted because it uses complex, standardized methods to produce results.

### The Ethics Audit: Legitimacy of Character
In contrast, an ethics audit is a more comprehensive and qualitative evaluation of an organization's moral health. It does not just look at the "what" (the numbers) but the "how" and the "why" (the character of the organization and its members).
* **Focus:** It systematically evaluates an organization’s ethical culture, its ethics programs, and the actual behavior of its employees compared to its stated values.
* **Goal:** The primary objective is to bolster **public trust** by ensuring that the profession is not just technically competent but also morally sound. 
* **Nature:** It moves toward what the authors call the **"legitimacy of character."** They argue that for decades, the accounting profession has overemphasized technique while neglecting the importance of personal and professional integrity.

### Key Differences in Priority
Abdolmohammadi and Nixon contend that while financial audits are necessary for legal compliance and market stability, they are no longer enough to maintain the profession's standing after major scandals like Enron. They suggest that the "legitimacy of technique" is insufficient if the public perceives the "character" of accountants to be flawed. By adopting ethics audits, accounting firms can proactively identify ethical risks and demonstrate a genuine commitment to the public good, rather than just "following the rules". Ultimately, the difference is between verifying that the **data is correct** (financial audit) and verifying that the **people and culture are ethical** (ethics audit).

In the early days of the profession, an accountant's value was rooted in their personal virtues and reputation. Because financial records were simpler, the public trusted the accountant as a person of high integrity. 2.  **The Loss of Public Trust:** When scandals like Enron occur, the public realizes that technical mastery can be used to deceive. Abdolmohammadi and Nixon argue that the accounting profession cannot regain trust simply by creating *more* complex rules (more technique). Instead, the profession must return to a "legitimacy of character."



From a **Deontological** perspective, an accountant has a duty to be truthful that transcends mere rule-following. From a **Virtue Ethics** perspective, being a professional requires the cultivation of honesty and courage, not just the memorization of a handbook. The authors conclude that for the profession to remain legitimate, it must integrate both: the accountant must be technically skilled, but those skills must be guided by a strong, underlying moral character. Without character, technique becomes nothing more than a tool for sophisticated deception.

## 8.6—Conflicting Services

The central problem with accounting firms offering non-audit services (such as management consulting or legal advice) to the same clients they are auditing is a profound **conflict of interest** that undermines the "public watchdog" function of the profession. This issue, which the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) sought to rectify, can be broken down into three primary ethical and structural concerns.

### 1. The Loss of Objectivity and Independence
The primary role of an external auditor is to provide an unbiased, third-party verification of a company's financial health. When an accounting firm also acts as a highly-paid consultant for that same company, the auditor's independence is compromised. The firm essentially becomes a "business partner" rather than a neutral judge. If the audit team discovers an error that resulted from advice given by their own firm's consulting arm, they face a massive incentive to hide that error to protect the firm’s reputation and lucrative consulting contracts.



### 2. Economic Dependence and "Auditor Capture"
Before SOX, consulting fees often dwarfed audit fees. This created a power imbalance where the accounting firm became economically dependent on the client's satisfaction. From a **Utilitarian** perspective, the "greatest good" (the stability of the financial market) was sacrificed for the "private good" of the firm's profit. If an auditor knows that a "qualified" (negative) audit opinion will result in the loss of millions of dollars in consulting revenue, the pressure to "look the other way" becomes almost insurmountable. This is known as "auditor capture," where the professional is effectively bought by the client.

### 3. Self-Review Threat
When a firm provides "conflicting services," they often end up auditing their own work. For example, if a firm’s consulting division designs a company's internal financial software and the audit division then tests that software for accuracy, they are unlikely to find or report flaws. This creates a "self-review threat" that violates the **Deontological** duty of honesty. A professional cannot be both the creator of a system and its objective critic.

### Normative Analysis
* **Contractarianism:** Rational members of society agree to give accountants a monopoly on auditing because they expect a "public watchdog." When firms offer conflicting services, they break this social contract by prioritizing private profit over the public's right to transparent data.
* **Virtue Ethics:** A professional must possess the virtue of **integrity**. By placing themselves in a position where their personal interest (consulting fees) conflicts with their professional duty (truthful auditing), they intentionally weaken their own ability to act with character.

In short, the problem was that accounting firms tried to serve two masters: the public (who require the truth) and the corporate client (who pays for growth). Sarbanes-Oxley recognized that these two interests are often fundamentally opposed, and that true "independence" requires a physical and financial separation of these services.
