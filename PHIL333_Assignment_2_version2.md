# The Absolute Duty to Serve: Re-evaluating Professional Monopolies in Law and Medicine

## Introduction

The ethical landscape of professional life is defined by a central tension: the personal moral beliefs of the professional versus the needs of the public they serve. A critical ethical issue arises when professionals, specifically lawyers and physicians, wish to withhold their services from those in need. While many argue for a middle ground that allows professionals to refuse service based on personal conscience or moral disgust, this paper will defend a much stricter position. Through a critical comparison of law and medicine, this paper argues that physicians and lawyers possess an almost absolute professional and moral obligation to provide their services. Because these professions hold a state-granted monopoly over essential human goods—health and justice—withholding service based on personal moral objections is ethically unjustified. By applying Contractarian, Utilitarian, and Deontological frameworks, this analysis will demonstrate why prioritizing the professional's personal conscience over the client or patient's need creates unacceptable ethical failures.



## The Contractarian Foundation of Absolute Duty

To determine whether a professional can withhold services, we must first look at how professions exist within society. According to Contractarian ethical theory, morality is based on mutual agreements or social contracts. The professions of law and medicine do not exist in a free market. Society grants these fields a strict monopoly. It is illegal to practice medicine or represent someone in court without a license. This monopoly gives professionals immense financial security, social status, and power. 

In exchange for this monopoly, the profession makes a promise to society: to provide essential services to all who need them. If an individual chooses to enter these professions and accept the benefits of the monopoly, they are simultaneously accepting the terms of the social contract. Therefore, the moral obligation to serve is not just a nice idea; it is the fundamental price of admission to the profession. From a Contractarian perspective, when a doctor or lawyer refuses to serve someone in need because of personal distaste or moral disagreement, they are breaking the core social contract that gives them their power in the first place.

## Withholding Medical Services: The Illusion of Conscientious Objection

In the medical field, the desire to withhold services often appears under the label of "conscientious objection." A physician might refuse to provide an abortion, prescribe contraceptives, or participate in medical assistance in dying (MAID) because it conflicts with their personal religious or moral beliefs. 

### Utilitarian and Deontological Failures
From a Utilitarian perspective, the primary goal of medicine is to maximize health and minimize suffering for the greatest number of people. Allowing doctors to withhold legal, standard medical procedures based on their own feelings causes direct harm. It forces vulnerable patients to search for new providers, delaying care, increasing anxiety, and in rural areas, potentially making care completely inaccessible. The utilitarian harm inflicted on the public far outweighs the psychological discomfort of the physician.

Furthermore, from a Deontological perspective, the core duty of a physician is to respect the autonomy and dignity of the patient. Immanuel Kant's categorical imperative requires us to treat people as ends in themselves. When a doctor refuses to provide a legal medical service because they personally disagree with the patient's choice, the doctor is elevating their own moral code over the patient's bodily autonomy. They are treating the patient merely as a bystander to the doctor's own moral purity. Therefore, a physician's moral obligation to provide needed medical care should override their personal desire to withhold it. 



## Withholding Legal Services: The Imperative of Representation

Lawyers frequently face the desire to withhold services when confronted with a "repugnant client." A lawyer might not want to defend an accused murderer, a known slumlord, or a corporation that pollutes the environment. The emotional and moral desire to step away from such cases is entirely human. 

### The Systemic Duty of the Lawyer
However, the legal system is an adversarial system. It is designed so that the truth is discovered and justice is served only when both sides have competent legal representation. If we look at this through a Utilitarian lens, a society where lawyers can broadly refuse to represent unpopular or "bad" people quickly becomes a society where the government can easily crush unpopular minorities. The overall utility of a fair, functioning justice system requires that even the worst members of society receive a vigorous defense.

Deontologically, a lawyer's duty is to the rule of law. A lawyer is an officer of the court. To deny legal representation to a person is to deny them their fundamental rights as a human being facing the power of the state. While a lawyer cannot be forced to lie or break the law for a client (as that destroys the system itself), refusing to simply *represent* a client because the lawyer finds their alleged actions immoral is a failure of professional duty. The lawyer's personal judgment of the client's character is irrelevant to the client's right to a defense.

## Critical Comparison: Similarities and Differences

When comparing lawyers and physicians under this strict interpretation of professional duty, several similarities and differences become clear.

### Similarities
1. **Monopoly over Vulnerability:** Both professions control access to fundamental human needs. A sick person cannot cure themselves, and an accused person cannot navigate the complex legal system alone. Both clients and patients are in states of extreme vulnerability.
2. **The Surrender of Personal Prejudice:** In both fields, the professional is required to set aside their personal prejudices. Just as a doctor must treat an injured criminal in the emergency room, a lawyer must ensure the criminal gets a fair trial. The professional's role is not to be the ultimate judge of the person's worth.

### Differences
1. **The Nature of the Service:** The physician provides a biological service that directly affects the physical reality of the patient. The lawyer provides a social service that mediates the relationship between the client and the state. 
2. **The Source of Harm:** When a doctor withholds service, the disease harms the patient. When a lawyer withholds service, the legal system itself (the state) harms the client. Because the state is a human-made institution, the lawyer's refusal to protect a citizen from the state carries a unique political weight that medical refusal does not.

## Rejecting the Alternative: The "Moral Agent" Defense

The most common alternative position—and the one this paper actively rejects—is the idea that professionals are "moral agents" first and professionals second. Proponents of this view argue that forcing a doctor to perform a procedure they find immoral, or forcing a lawyer to defend a repugnant client, violates the professional's human rights and causes them severe moral injury.

### Rebuttal to the Moral Agent Defense
This position is less satisfactory from an ethical perspective because it fundamentally misunderstands the nature of choosing a profession. Individuals are not drafted into medicine or law; they choose these paths voluntarily. 

When a person chooses to become a physician or a lawyer, they are voluntarily adopting a specific set of Deontological duties. They are making a promise to society. If an individual holds personal moral beliefs that prevent them from providing standard, legal medical procedures, or prevents them from defending unpopular clients, they should not enter those specific areas of the profession. A person whose conscience forbids them from providing contraceptives should not become a family physician, just as a pacifist should not join the military. 

Prioritizing the professional's moral comfort over the public's access to health and justice shifts the burden of the moral dilemma onto the most vulnerable party—the sick patient or the accused citizen. This is a profound ethical failure. The burden of professional choice must rest on the professional.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, a rigorous ethical analysis reveals that physicians and lawyers possess a near-absolute moral and professional obligation to provide their services to those in need. The privileges, wealth, and power granted to these professions by society's Contractarian agreements demand an equally massive sacrifice of personal preference. While it is uncomfortable for a physician to perform a procedure they personally dislike, or for a lawyer to defend a client they find morally repulsive, these discomforts are the required cost of the professional monopoly. Utilitarian principles demand that the public have reliable access to health and justice, and Deontological principles demand that professionals fulfill the strict duties they voluntarily assumed. Therefore, situations where professionals wish to withhold services based on personal conscience should be viewed not as a valid exercise of professional autonomy, but as a breach of the fundamental trust placed in them by society.
